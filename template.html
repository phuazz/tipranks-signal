Liquid universe — the panel

Action — the default order — answers one question per name: what did analysts do about this stock since last week? ▲ they moved in its favour — upgraded the consensus, raised the best-analyst price target, or lifted the composite score · ▼ they moved against it · · they did nothing. The number says how much happened at once and how verifiable it is: ±9–10 strong — rating change and target change together, verified by a dated rating inside the week (the week's strongest cluster) · ±4–8 clear — one meaningful action · ±1–3 faint — only the composite ticked, or no dated rating backs the move. It measures action, not quality: a strong company where analysts did nothing this week scores 0 and sinks down this table. Why it leads: changes in analyst opinion are the one signal in this data with published evidence behind it — levels are already priced in. Hover a badge for that name's own story, click the row for the full profile; exact weights sit in the column tooltip and the whole window on the Revision Monitor tab. Ties break by rating freshness, then upside ÷ vol. Smart Score is TipRanks' 1–10 composite of eight inputs — analyst consensus, blogger opinion, insider activity, hedge-fund activity, news sentiment, crowd sentiment, technicals and fundamentals — proprietary weights, 10 most bullish; a column and a filter here, not the order, because levels are the weakest evidence layer. Hide trap profiles removes names whose high implied upside sits on a deteriorating profile: 3-month return below the universe median, net insider selling, or Smart Score ≤ 4 — view-only, ungraded. How to read the panel — the lens order

The view-layer organisation the evidence supports, applied in the Liquid Universe table. Ungraded by construction.
  • 1 · Gate the traps first. Hide price-falling names (3-month TR below the universe median), negative insider signals, and Smart Score ≤ 4. High implied upside on that profile is usually a stale target on a deteriorating name, not a buy. S3 gate logic
  • 2 · Rank within sector, not across the market. Implied upside carries information only relative to sector peers, so the default sort is the sector-relative percentile of best-analyst upside per unit of volatility (Best↑/σ). view lens
  • 3 · From snapshot 2, freshness takes over. Week-on-week upgrades and best-analyst target raises become the primary sort; the levels-based lens above is only the best available interim ordering while one capture exists. S1a · S1b
  • 4 · Sectors: watch revision breadth, not level. Net upgrades and target-raise share by sector (staged on the Revision Monitor). Sector mean Smart Score is a levels read and carries little. diagnostic

Revisions beat levels

  • Womack (1996), Journal of Finance. Recommendation changes are followed by drift, asymmetrically — downgrade drift is larger and longer-lived than upgrade drift. S1a primary · short-side diagnostic
  • Barber, Lehavy, McNichols & Trueman (2001), Journal of Finance. Consensus-level portfolios earn gross abnormal returns that transaction costs erase; turnover is the killer. tiered costs, every rebalance
  • Sell-side information study (2024, working paper). Revision reports carry roughly six times the information of reiterations (out-of-sample R² ≈ 21–23% vs ≈ 3.6%); confirmatory rating-plus-target pairs reinforce, contradictory pairs cancel. reiterations excluded
  • Loh & Stulz (2011), Review of Financial Studies. Only ~12% of recommendation changes are influential, concentrated in reputable analysts acting away from consensus. best-analyst weighting

Price targets: biased level, usable relative signal

  • Brav & Lehavy (2003), Journal of Finance. Target-price revisions move prices — information events even conditional on the rating. S1b
  • Da & Schaumburg (2011), Journal of Financial Markets. Target-implied upside predicts within sector, not across the market; the sector-neutral construction earned ~2%/month over 1999–2004 — an in-sample-era upper bound. sector-relative rank
  • Dechow & You (2020), The Accounting Review. The largest component of implied returns is analyst error about risk; roughly 16–18 cents per dollar of implied upside is realised, and only on claimed undervaluation. raw upside never a sort key
  • “Betting against analyst target price” (2021), Journal of Financial Markets. The highest-implied-return names underperform — the optimism tail inverts. trap gate
  • Dispersion and consensus targets, Management Science. Implied returns predict when analyst disagreement is low and invert when it is high. B−C gap column

Corroboration axes

  • Cohen, Malloy & Pomorski (2012), Journal of Finance. Opportunistic insider trades predict returns; routine ones do not. The insider axis is the most orthogonal own-money corroborator of an analyst signal. S3 gate · S2 component
  • House result — event-studies funnel (2026-07). The deployability stress-test collapsed 14 discovered leads to 1 once drift-adjusted: the apparent edge was beta plus high-drift-name selection. The value-trap anti-pattern (big upside, weak price, low composite) is the single-name version of the same failure. drift-adjusted headline · price gate
  • Composite recursion guard. The Smart Score embeds the analyst consensus, so gating a revision on Smart-Score-rising would partially confirm the signal with itself; the score is graded once, as-is (S1c), and its sub-axes serve attribution only. S1c · no double-counting

Discipline: why the menu is frozen

  • McLean & Pontiff (2016), Journal of Finance. Published anomaly returns decay by roughly half post-publication — expectations for any analyst-signal edge should be modest and cost-fragile. realistic prize
  • Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016), Review of Financial Studies; Bailey & López de Prado (2014). Multiple testing inflates discovery; haircuts and the deflated Sharpe ratio are the correction. N = 5 · BH-FDR q = 0.10 · DSR
  • Pre-registration (this project). Forward-only snapshots; five schemes frozen before the second capture; ratchet, clock-start and no-promotion bookkeeping; verdicts only from ≥ 26 captures. register 3–5

What the Smart Score is

TipRanks' proprietary 1–10 composite. Components as documented by the vendor; weights undisclosed.
  • Eight inputs: analyst consensus · blogger opinion · insider transactions · hedge-fund activity (13F-based, quarterly — stale at weekly cadence) · news sentiment · crowd (individual-investor) sentiment · technicals (momentum) · fundamentals (return on equity, asset growth). vendor composite
  • The vendor claim — top-decile Smart Score outperforms — is tested once, as-is, as scheme S1c on the frozen menu. The score embeds the analyst consensus, so it never gates another analyst signal (recursion guard); its exposed sub-axes in the export (insider / hedge-fund / news / sentiment) serve as attribution of any S1c grade and are never graded themselves. S1c · attribution only
